Teams often mistake a conversation with users for user research.

On fast-moving projects, there is usually a lot of pressure to start building _the thing_. This usually leads to an eager developer or member of the delivery team sitting down with a user and asking, _"What do you want?"_, getting a list of requirements, and then building exactly what they asked for.

Good job, right? You've listened to the user and built exactly what they said they want. That must be user research.

No. It's not.

## What's the problem?

I've talked previously about how [users are experts in their own frustrations](/writing/user-sign-off-is-not-product-accountability/), but our users also have a tendency to think about solutions in terms of the tools they already use and the processes they already follow.

This is **Familiarity Bias**. We are more likely to prefer known, familiar solutions to unfamiliar solutions.

If we take the example of a user who is frustrated with a slow, clunky process, and we build exactly what they request, chances are that the solution will address the symptom but leave the root problem untouched.
